The Service Delivery Journey

Navigate through each critical step of the wheelchair acquisition process while exploring diverse stakeholder perspectives

01

Initial Assessment

Get in-touch with medical front office staff to schedule an appointment The client needs to contact the clinic's administrative staff to schedule a consultation for obtaining a prescription.

Front office staff help coordinate the appropriate appointment type based on the client's needs or referral. The staff then coordinate with the clinician's schedule to book the client for a consultation.

02

Prescription & Approval

A physician (e.g., primary care, physiatrist) evaluates the client’s medical condition and writes a referral for a wheelchair assessment. This referral is typically required by insurers to initiate the seating and mobility evaluation process.

03

Selecting the Right Fit

The client collaborates with a physical or occupational therapist and a certified Assistive Technology Professional (ATP) usually from the supplier. Through one or more evaluation sessions, they assess posture, functional needs, and daily activities. Various wheelchair bases and seating components are trialed, and the team documents clinical justifications A clinical justification is a written explanation by a healthcare professional describing why a specific wheelchair and components are medically necessary for the client’s daily function and quality of life. for the final recommendation.

04

Prescription to Authorization

After the final wheelchair configuration is determined, the physician provides a prescription, and the clinician prepares supporting documentation such as the Letter of Medical Necessity. The supplier compiles these with insurer-specific forms and submits the full packet for prior authorization. Communication delays are common; some clients or caregivers proactively check approval status with their insurer and relay updates to the supplier to help move the process forward.

05

Navigating Paperwork & Approvals

While clinicians and suppliers are responsible for submitting documentation, wheelchair users and caregivers often must repeatedly follow up with insurers or providers to track paperwork and approvals, adding stress to an already lengthy process.

X

Insurance Denial

When a denial is received, the supplier and clinician reviews the reasons and determines next steps. Funding bodies may provide limited feedback. A blanket denial states the individual does not qualify for the equipment, while a component denial targets a specific part of the wheelchair rather than the entire device.This prevents wheelchair user to get their chairs covered by payors, and have to either give-up on acquisition or pay out-of-pocket.

06

Equipment Fulfillment

Once authorization is secured, the supplier orders the wheelchair and components from the manufacturer. At delivery, the supplier (and often the clinician) ensure the equipment matches the prescription and fits the client appropriately. After delivery, the supplier coordinates warranty service, adjustments, and repairs with the manufacturer as needed.

Research Insights

Our comprehensive survey of 62 stakeholders across the wheelchair service delivery ecosystem revealed critical insights that supplement the stakeholder stories. These data visualizations highlight systemic patterns and qualitative experiences shared throughout this journey.

[Survey Chart]

Systemic Communication Breakdown

Communication emerged as the most frequently cited barrier (71% of respondents), with stakeholders across all roles reporting fragmented information flow between users, clinicians, suppliers, and insurers. The average satisfaction with communication quality was only 3.9/7, indicating significant room for improvement.

[Survey Chart]

Insurance Approval as Central Bottleneck

Insurance approval was identified as a critical pain point by 58% of respondents, with particularly low satisfaction scores (avg 3.8/7) for the current timeline. Payers themselves acknowledged the complexity, with one noting that "depending on an individual's plan, they may or may not be approved for a wheelchair" due to varying medical necessity criteria.

[Survey Chart]

Stakeholder Misalignment on Priorities

Wheelchair users prioritized customization and communication equally. Clinicians focused heavily on insurance approval challenges. Suppliers/Manufacturers emphasized delivery timeline issues. Caregivers showed concern across all areas, particularly communication gaps.

[Survey Chart]

Future Vision Divergence

Responses to future service delivery models showed a split: 40% hoped for more user-centered, streamlined processes by 2035. 35% expected minimal change from current systems. 25% envisioned technology-enabled transformations.

Vision 1: The User-Empowered Direct Model
[Sample]
In this future, wheelchair users have direct access to manufacturers through AI-powered platforms that bypass traditional intermediaries. Users complete virtual assessments using motion capture and AR technology, receive personalized equipment recommendations within 24 hours, and can trial equipment through VR simulations. The entire process—from initial request to delivery—takes 30 days or less.

Key features:
  • AI-driven documentation that eliminates bureaucratic barriers
  • Direct user-manufacturer relationships with embedded clinical consultation
  • Blockchain-verified funding approvals processed in real-time
  • Predictive maintenance using IoT sensors that prevent breakdowns
  • User-controlled data sharing that empowers informed decision-making
×
Vision 2: The Integrated Care Network
[Sample]
This model envisions a fully integrated ecosystem where all stakeholders—users, clinicians, suppliers, insurers, and manufacturers—operate on a unified platform. Real-time data sharing eliminates communication gaps, while AI orchestrates the entire process, reducing timeline from 110+ days to under 45 days.

Key features:
  • Single portal with transparent tracking for all stakeholders
  • AI-powered care coordinators that proactively manage each case
  • Automated insurance pre-approvals based on clinical algorithms
  • Regional "wheelchair hubs" offering same-day repairs and loaner equipment
  • Continuous feedback loops that improve system performance
×
Vision 3: The Preventive Care Evolution
[Sample]
This future prioritizes prevention over reaction, with wheelchairs as smart health devices that continuously monitor user wellbeing. Regular virtual check-ins, predictive maintenance, and modular designs that adapt to changing needs eliminate the traditional 5-year replacement cycle.

Key features:
  • Quarterly wellness assessments combining physical and equipment health
  • Modular wheelchairs that evolve with user needs
  • Community-based repair cooperatives with user-trained technicians
  • Insurance models rewarding preventive maintenance
  • Telehealth integration for real-time clinical support
